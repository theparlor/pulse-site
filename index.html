Judgment & discovery

The signal from the people you follow, distilled daily.

Pulse watches the publishers behind your watch-list — at their actual cadence, not a coarse sweep — and delivers a tiered morning brief ranked against your interests. No notifications to scroll. No feeds to triage. Just what matters, in the order it matters.

The problem it solves

Scrolling to find the five things worth reading is not a reading strategy.

High-velocity thinkers — the ones who publish daily, who respond to events in real time — get buried under notification noise before you reach them. Generic reader apps rank by recency. Pulse ranks by relevance to you: your active work, your open questions, your interest profile. The brief that lands at 6:30 AM already knows that Willison's post today is Tier 1 and the newsletter you subscribe to out of habit is Tier 4.

The most valuable signal isn't the loudest. It's the one that matches exactly what you're working on right now.
How you use it

Watch the voices. Read the brief.

1

Build your watch-list

Point Pulse at the thinkers whose work you want to follow — engineers, strategists, practitioners. Each persona's publishing channels are tracked at their actual cadence: daily, weekly, monthly.

2

Set your interest profile

Core themes, active engagements, open questions. The scoring model weighs what you're working on today — not a static topic list. A five-year-old Cagan essay surfaces as Tier 1 if it matches your live context.

3

Read the four tiers

Tier 1: full synopsis + verbatim hook quote. Tier 2: one-line read. Tier 3: headline to pin. Tier 4: logged, suppressed. Brien decides in seconds what deserves minutes.

What makes it different

The conservation law: author voice is never paraphrased away.

The rule the whole product is built around

A synopsis without the author's words is not a synopsis.

Most digest tools collapse a post to a summary. Pulse refuses at Tier 1: every must-read item carries a verbatim hook quote alongside the editorial synopsis. The synopsis can be yours; the quote is theirs. And when two tracked publishers disagree on the same topic — Willison vs. Karpathy on AI engineering practice, say — Pulse surfaces both with a disagreement pointer, per the same dissent-preservation law that governs Voices. The two output channels:

Ranked digest

The tiered brief — Tier 1 through Tier 4 — ordered by relevance score, not recency. What to read and in what order.

Cross-publisher dissent

Where tracked voices disagree on the same topic, both items surface with a cross-pointer. The disagreement is the signal — never collapsed.

How Pulse was forged.

Pulse wasn't designed to solve a generic reading problem. It was built to solve the specific cadence-mismatch and relevance-triage failures in Brien's actual morning workflow — governed by the same operating model and persona registry that power every product in this portfolio.

The operating model

Intent

Pulse carries its own .intent/ governance — signals, specs, decisions, traced in git. The verbatim-quote invariant and cross-publisher dissent law are ratified specs enforced by the pipeline. Channel cadence, interest profile, and scoring model are all governed, not hardcoded. Pulse reads the Cast registry read-only; it never writes back.
